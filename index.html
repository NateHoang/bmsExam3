    Lecture 9: Vitamins Macronutrients
    Macronutrient Initial Digestion (Mouth/Stomach) Final Digestion (Small Intestine) Absorption Mechanism Transport Destination
    Carbohydrates Salivary amylase in the mouth begins breaking down starches into smaller polysaccharides. Pancreatic amylase and brush border enzymes (disaccharidases) break them into monosaccharides (glucose, fructose, galactose). Absorbed as monosaccharides via sodium-dependent transporters (SGLT-1) and GLUT transporters. Portal circulation (to the liver).
    Proteins
  • Pepsin and Hydrochloric acid (HCl) in the stomach begin denaturing and cleaving proteins.
  • Pepsinogen → Pepsin → Peptones
    		•  Pancreatic peptidases (trypsin, chymotrypsin) and membrane-bound aminopeptidases break them into amino acids and di/tripeptides.
  • Absorbed by enterocytes via sodium-hydrogen exchangers (NHE) for amino acids and peptide transporter 1 (PepT1) for di/tripeptides.
  • Amino acids aminopeptidases located on plasma membrane of interstinal cells (enterocytes) finalize the peptide digestion to amino acids
    		• Portal circulation.
    Lipids (Fats) Lingual lipase in the mouth begins minor breakdown.
  • Bile acids emulsify lipids in SI and increase SA for digestion
  • Pancreatic lipase hydrolyze triglyceride lipids to fatty acids, monoglycerides and cholesterol
  • Bile solubilize those products in micelles to prepare for absorption
  • Dietary triacylglycerols emulsified by bile salts and digested by pancreatic lipases to fatty acids and glycerol
  • Bile acids micelles around monoglycerides and free fatty acids to facilitate their diffusion to enterocytes of the jejunum and proximal ileum
  • In intestinal mucosa, fatty acids are resynthesized into triacylglycerols, packaged into chylomicrons
    		• Lymphatic system (chyle) then into the blood; small FAs go to portal blood.

    Micronutrient Category Specific Nutrient Common Sources Primary Functions Absorption Mechanism & Site
    Fat-Soluble Vitamins Vitamin A (Retinol) Liver, eggs, fish Vision, epithelial cell differentiation Incorporated into micelles; absorbed from the intestine with lipids.
    Vitamin D Fortified milk, meat Calcium and phosphorus homeostasis Incorporated into micelles; absorbed with lipids.
    Vitamin E (Tocopherols) Green vegetables Antioxidant Incorporated into micelles; absorbed with lipids.
    Vitamin K - - Incorporated into micelles; absorbed with lipids.
    Water-Soluble Vitamins Vitamin B Complex (B1, B2, B3, B6, B9, B12, Biotin, Pantothenic acid) (Varies by type) Energy metabolism, cofactors, hematopoiesis (B9, B12) Absorbed via Sodium-dependent transport mechanisms in the intestine.

    Minerals & Electrolytes Calcium - Bone/tooth mineralization Requires Vitamin D; absorbed through the small intestine.
    Iron - Hemoglobin synthesis Absorbed in the ferrous state from the duodenum.
    Folate (B9) - Hematopoiesis Absorbed from the proximal jejunum.
    Magnesium Green leafy vegetables, legumes, nuts, seeds Cofactor for biochemical reactions, energy production Mainly digested in intestines
    Zinc Meat, fish, seafood, beans, nuts Cellular metabolism, immune function, DNA/protein synthesis Mainly digested in intestines

    Vitamin B's
    Vitamin Name Primary Function Deficiency Symptoms Oral Manifestations
    B1 Thiamine
  • Energy homeostasis
  • Critical for a cell's ability to generate energy
    		•  Beriberi (neurological, cardiovascular, and musculature issues). Associated with the severity of periodontal disease.
    B2 Riboflavin Cofactor in oxidation and reduction metabolic reactions. Scaly skin (scrotum, labia, nose). Glossitis (red, sore tongue), angular cheilitis (cracked lip corners), red throat, dry lips.
    B3 Niacin
  • Converted to NAD
  • Required for electron transfer in carbohydrate and lipid metabolism.
    		• Pellagra (the "3 Ds": Diarrhea, Dermatitis, Dementia); can be fatal. Glossitis and stomatitis (can progress to ulcers and infections).
    B6 Pyridoxal Co-enzyme for amino acid synthesis and production of catecholamines.
  • Dermatitis and anemia
  • Deficiency rare but seen with isoniazid.
    		•  Glossitis.
    B9 Folate Coenzyme for nucleotide synthesis, DNA repair, and RBC growth. Megaloblastic anemia and birth defects (neural tube defects). Glossitis.
    B12 Cyanocobalamin Hemoglobin synthesis, protein, and fat metabolism. Pernicious anemia (due to B12 malabsorption), pallor, and fatigue. Glossitis, angular cheilitis, and aphthous-like ulcerations.
